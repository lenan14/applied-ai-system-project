# Bird and Rabbit Care Guidelines

## Rabbit Feeding

Rabbits require unlimited timothy hay as their primary food source, making up 80 percent of their diet. Hay supports digestive motility and dental health by wearing down continuously growing teeth. Fresh leafy greens such as romaine lettuce, cilantro, and parsley can be offered daily in small amounts. Pellets should be limited to 1/4 cup per 5 pounds of body weight per day. Fresh water must always be available.

## Rabbit Exercise

Rabbits need a minimum of 3 to 4 hours of free-roaming exercise each day outside their enclosure. Exercise prevents obesity and the dangerous condition called gastrointestinal stasis, where gut motility slows and can be fatal if untreated. Enrichment items such as tunnels, cardboard boxes, and chewable toys encourage natural foraging behavior.

## Rabbit Health Monitoring

Signs of gastrointestinal stasis include reduced or absent fecal pellets, loss of appetite, and a hunched posture. This is a veterinary emergency requiring immediate treatment. Unlike dogs and cats, rabbits cannot vomit, making gut blockages life-threatening. Spaying or neutering reduces the risk of reproductive cancers, which affect a high percentage of unspayed female rabbits.

## Bird Feeding

Pet birds such as budgies, cockatiels, and parrots should not subsist on seed alone. A varied diet including formulated pellets, fresh vegetables, and limited fruit provides balanced nutrition. Avocado, chocolate, caffeine, and onion are toxic to birds and must be avoided. Fresh water should be changed daily to prevent bacterial contamination.

## Bird Enrichment

Birds are highly intelligent and require daily out-of-cage time and social interaction. Foraging toys, mirrors, bells, and rotating novel items prevent boredom and feather-destructive behavior. Most parrot species need 2 to 4 hours of out-of-cage interaction daily. Lack of enrichment is the primary cause of behavioral problems in captive birds.

## Bird Health Monitoring

Birds naturally hide signs of illness; by the time symptoms are apparent, the bird may already be seriously ill. Monitor for changes in droppings, reduced vocalization, puffed feathers, or loss of appetite. Annual wellness exams with an avian veterinarian are recommended. Keep birds away from kitchen fumes, especially non-stick cookware, which releases fumes toxic to birds.
