# Senior Pet Care Guidelines

## Age Thresholds

Dogs are generally considered senior at age 7 or older, though large breeds age faster and may be senior at 5 to 6 years. Cats are considered senior at age 10 to 11. Senior pets have different nutritional, medical, and mobility needs compared to younger animals.

## Veterinary Monitoring

Senior pets should visit a veterinarian every 6 months rather than the standard annual schedule. Bloodwork panels detect early kidney disease, diabetes, hyperthyroidism in cats, and hypothyroidism in dogs before symptoms appear. Early detection dramatically improves treatment outcomes and quality of life.

## Exercise Modifications

Older pets still need daily exercise but intensity and duration should be reduced. Shorter, more frequent walks are preferable to one long session for dogs with arthritis or joint stiffness. Low-impact activities like swimming are ideal for dogs with hip dysplasia. Avoid strenuous activity in extreme heat or cold.

## Nutrition

Senior pets require diets formulated for their age group, with adjusted calorie levels, increased protein for muscle maintenance, and joint supplements such as glucosamine and omega-3 fatty acids. Digestive efficiency decreases with age, so smaller and more frequent meals may aid absorption.

## Pain and Mobility Management

Arthritis is common in senior dogs and cats. Watch for reduced activity, reluctance to climb stairs, or difficulty rising from rest. Orthopedic beds, ramps, and non-slip surfaces reduce joint stress. Medications such as NSAIDs should only be given under veterinary supervision.

## Cognitive Health

Canine cognitive dysfunction and feline cognitive dysfunction are similar to dementia in humans. Signs include disorientation, altered sleep cycles, and forgetting house training. Consistent daily routines reduce anxiety and disorientation in cognitively affected senior pets.
